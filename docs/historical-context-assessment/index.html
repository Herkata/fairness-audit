Overview and Guidanceâ€‹

This tool guides you through a structured assessment of the historical context relevant to your AI application. By systematically examining the domain, data, features, and model choices through the lens of documented patterns of discrimination, you can identify potential risks and vulnerabilities that may arise from replicating or amplifying these patterns.

Questionnaire Sectionsâ€‹

1. Domainâ€‹

What domain is your application aimed at?

e.g. Healthcare, Jurisdiction, Advertisement, Production

How old is this branch, and what influenced its development throughout history?

e.g. Industrial monopolies, Political directives, Legislation

Which kinds of discrimination have been recorded in this domain?

e.g. Black women in space research, ageism in hiring, women earning less

Is explainability an important factor in your field?

e.g. Loans, recidivism, CT scans

2. Application Conceptâ€‹

What is your applicationâ€™s function/goal?

e.g. Scoring, categorization, risk assessment

Is there an existing, comparable solution that either leads by example or illustrates practices to avoid?

e.g. Facial recognition bias against Black women, image generation showing only white persons

Based on the information gathered above, what are your applicationâ€™s vulnerabilities or weak spots?

e.g. Bias, amplifying implicit discrimination, insufficient data

3. Dataâ€‹

What is the data source you plan to use?

e.g. Public, limited access, owned by your company

Is the data source well-documented?

Are the features, schema, and collection process documented?

Has the data been collected ethically and legally?

Is the data from a legitimate source? Was there a leak or unethical practice?

Is the data representative of the population it was collected from?

Does the collected data represent the whole collection group accurately? e.g. A university poll considering students and staff

Is the data representative of the population it is going to make predictions on?

Where is the application going to be used? Are the target group and collection group comparable?

Which variables have been measured?
What kinds of scales have been applied?
Have data collection practices changed since the data were recorded?

4. Featuresâ€‹

Which features are included in training, and why?
Define which attributes are/should be protected to avoid discrimination.

e.g. Race, gender, social status

Run correlation analysis to identify proxies for protected attributes.

e.g. Address for social status, name for ethnicity

Does any feature definition disadvantage certain groups?

e.g. Physical status, leadership fitness, employment continuity

Does the encoding of attributes disadvantage certain groups?

e.g. 0, 1, 2 for male, female, other

Are categorization practices discriminatory or derogatory?

e.g. Limited gender options, inaccurate race options

5. Intersectionality Findingsâ€‹

Are there intersectional subgroups that require further exploration based on historical context?

e.g. Women of color in scientific fields, low-income Indigenous communities in political representation, women of childbearing age in hiring

6. Model Selectionâ€‹

What priorities were set in historical applications with the same goal?
Which models achieved these goals, and what downsides did they have?
Which technical performance metrics will you use to choose the final model or ensemble, and why?
Is your model explainable? Which features contribute to the results and how?
Which factors are used in hyperparameter tuning?
Do these factors increase the influence of protected variables?

Introduce fairness metrics applicable to your solution.

Risk Classification Matrixâ€‹

This matrix assesses risks from historically documented patterns of discrimination relevant to an internal loan application system. In line with the EU AI Act, particular attention is given to risks affecting access to credit, classified as a high-risk use case due to its impact on economic opportunity and fundamental rights.

Matrix Dimensionsâ€‹

Historical Pattern

A well-documented historical pattern of discrimination or exclusion, supported by legal, social, or empirical evidence, particularly in financial and credit-related contexts.

Impact Severity â€“ Potential harm if the historical pattern is reproduced by the system:

  • High (3): Affects fundamental rights or critical life outcomes (e.g. access to housing, credit, economic stability)
  • Medium (2): Produces significant inequality in access to financial resources or economic opportunity
  • Low (1): Leads to differential treatment with limited material impact

Occurrence Likelihood â€“ Probability that the pattern may manifest in AI-based loan systems:

  • High (3): Frequently observed in historical or contemporary credit systems
  • Medium (2): Documented but context-dependent
  • Low (1): Rare or indirect

System Relevance â€“ Applicability to the internal loan application system:

  • High (3): Directly related to creditworthiness assessment, risk scoring, or approval decisions
  • Medium (2): Indirectly influences decision-support components
  • Low (1): Peripheral relevance

Risk Priority Score

Calculated as: Impact Severity + Occurrence Likelihood + System Relevance
